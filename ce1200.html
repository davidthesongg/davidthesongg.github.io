1200 C.E.
Black Death

During the Black Death outbreak in Europe, which lasted from 1347 to 1351, cats were widely believed to be responsible for spreading the disease.

This belief was based on superstitions and misinformation, rather than scientific evidence. As a result, many cats were killed in an attempt to prevent the spread of the disease.

However, this only served to exacerbate the problem, as cats were natural predators of rats, which were the actual carriers of the fleas that transmitted the disease.

The killing of cats led to an increase in the rat population, which in turn led to the further spread of the disease.

It wasn't until much later that it was recognized that the Black Death was caused by the bacterium Yersinia pestis, which was spread by fleas that infested rats.
